## I - Keeping Up With The Jones'

My favorite business book from last year was Yongme Moon's [Different](https://www.amazon.com/Different-Escaping-Competitive-Youngme-Moon/dp/030746086X). It was sort of a serendipitous find, I stumbled across the book while reading through [Andrew Chen's old blog posts](https://andrewchen.com/ten-classic-books-that-define-tech/). The book is, well, different: it's a lot less structured than most business books which I think actually greatly benefits the readability. Most business books are highly organized summaries of usually rather dubious bodies of organizational psychology research that come away with sweeping "science-backed" insights. Different just feels like having a conversation with a smart person on some pet theory of hers.

One idea that has managed to live rent-free inside of my head since I read it is something Moon calls "conspicuous competition". This is the competitive analogue to conspicuous consumption, which is more commonly known as "keeping up with the Jones'".

The rough way it works is that companies don't want their competitors to get a leg up. So every time a company pulls ahead on something that could potentially differentiate them, other companies follow, and it quickly ceases to be notable. In fact, you can sort of think of this as a competitive regression towards the mean.

Moon gives an example of Volvo. Volvo was once – and perhaps is still – well known for being the safest car on the market. And so buying a Volvo meant you were getting an exceptionally safe car from a company that was uniquely obsessed with building safe cars. But an interesting thing happened. When Volvo looked at what their customers wanted, they were asking for things like "make the car sportier". So Volvo, like any good business, started to make improvements to the sportiness of their cars. Circa 2010 when *Different* was published, Moon claims that Volvo had succeeded, they're pretty clearly one of many premium car brands, like Audi, Mercedes, and Lexus, but in terms of safety, Audi now (or in 2010) makes the safer car.

Whereas before there was no reason for a safety obsessed buyer to consider any car but a Volvo, now there's basically no obvious reason to consider a Volvo over any other car in the same category at all. It's completely undifferentiated.

Moon argues pretty convincingly that most major brands are like this. Can most people honestly tell the difference between name brand and off-brand grocery items? Do most people have a large preference between a stay in a Hilton hotel or a Hyatt hotel? There are categories in airlines but between categories, I don't really care if I fly Spirit or Frontier, or if I fly Delta or American. Airlines can buy loyalty with rewards programs, but they all pretty much look the same, so the airline you're loyal to is much more an issue of path-dependency than an issue of the intrinsic merits of American over Delta.

An interesting thing here is, if most major brands are like this, then it's not 100% obvious it's bad. I mean, they all survive don't they? But I still can't help think of Thiel's play on Tolstoy:

*"All unhappy companies are alike; all happy companies happy in their own way"*.

Yet we also see some brands that are truly differentiated, even in markets that could roughly be considered commodity markets. And this is the topic of *Different*. Brands like Ikea, and Red Bull have managed to stand out by doing things very differently from their competitors. For Red Bull, it was being content to let rumors spread; Red Bull's best marketers were school teachers telling their students how unhealthy Red Bull is, and rumors about it using ingredients like bull testicles which they deliberately chose to not correct. For Ikea, they provided certain services that no other furniture store did (e.g. childcare) while simultaneously cutting out all the other luxuries. Ikea furniture doesn't last a lifetime, Ikea doesn't deliver (except they do now). etc. [1]

## II - Entropy

Jeff Bezos has a section in his final shareholder letter at Amazon that instantly resonated with me. It feels worth quoting at length:

> Here is a passage from Richard Dawkins’ (extraordinary) book The Blind Watchmaker. It’s about a basic fact of biology.
> 
> “Staving off death is a thing that you have to work at. Left to itself – and that is what it is when it dies – the body tends to revert to a state of equilibrium with its environment. If you measure some quantity such as the temperature, the acidity, the water content or the electrical potential in a living body, you will typically find that it is markedly different from the corresponding measure in the surroundings. Our bodies, for instance, are usually hotter than our surroundings, and in cold climates they have to work hard to maintain the differential. When we die the work stops, the temperature differential starts to disappear, and we end up the same temperature as our surroundings. Not all animals work so hard to avoid coming into equilibrium with their surrounding temperature, but all animals do some comparable work. For instance, in a dry country, animals and plants work to maintain the fluid content of their cells, work against a natural tendency for water to flow from them into the dry outside world. If they fail they die. More generally, if living things didn’t work actively to prevent it, they would eventually merge into their surroundings, and cease to exist as autonomous beings. That is what happens when they die.”
> 
> While the passage is not intended as a metaphor, it’s nevertheless a fantastic one, and very relevant to Amazon. I would argue that it’s relevant to all companies and all institutions and to each of our individual lives too. In what ways does the world pull at you in an attempt to make you normal? How much work does it take to maintain your distinctiveness? To keep alive the thing or things that make you special?
> 
> ...
> 
> We all know that distinctiveness – originality – is valuable. We are all taught to “be yourself.” What I’m really asking you to do is to embrace and be realistic about how much energy it takes to maintain that distinctiveness. The world wants you to be typical – in a thousand ways, it pulls at you. Don’t let it happen.
> 
> You have to pay a price for your distinctiveness, and it’s worth it. The fairy tale version of “be yourself” is that all the pain stops as soon as you allow your distinctiveness to shine. That version is misleading. Being yourself is worth it, but don’t expect it to be easy or free. You’ll have to put energy into it continuously.

This seems to be another broader way to frame conspicuous competition. One that encompasses both conspicuous competition and conspicuous consumption. Both are just special cases of entropy.

What I find interesting about this view is that entropy is a force of nature. Being undifferentiated is the default and it's something you have to constantly fight against to maintain your identity.

## III - Moats Are For Investors

I used to play guitar in a metal band, and I still record metal music. Virtual instruments have become amazingly good these days, and so you don't really need to be able to play guitar to write really good metal music. In theory the best metal artists could now all be producers who have never touched a guitar. This isn't the case. Why not?

Some of it is branding, it's just a lot cooler if someone played things on a guitar. But some of it I think goes to the core of what metal music *is*. When I write music, my process very often looks like this: set up my recording software, plug my guitar in, and just play what comes to mind. Then when I play something cool, I refine it a little, and hit record. Then I go on to the next riff.

I have a lot of habits when I play. And so I can write a riff even if I don't have a pre-existing idea. My body just moves. I think it's actually the habits in my hands that make my music sound like it's *my music*. No one else has those exact same habits. The metal guitar genre as a whole is probably best seen as a superset of these habits.

It's not just me either. For example, I can always recognize an ERRA song even if it's brand new. The riffs just sound like ERRA. I'm not sure exactly how Jesse writes his riffs, but I suspect there's similar effects where years of experience playing guitar turn into habits that form his fingerprint.

This doesn't mean you can't write music other ways. There are many distinct EDM artists with their own unmistakable sound too. But again, I'd guess these are due to some type of habits they've built along the way too. But if you want to write metal, it pays to have built *guitar* habits yourself.

This is tacit knowledge and I think it's one way of moat building. There's nothing new about this either. Tacit knowledge is hard to describe, and so it's hard for an AI or another human to copy.

This ties into a lot of discussion I have seen recently on LinkedIn, Twitter and in the blogosphere about moats. If AI is drastically reducing the cost of development, it's suddenly very easy to create features and to copy the features of competitors. And this means the old time moat of having been there first with a technically complex product is much shallower than before. It's just much easier to catch up, and the cost of *discovering* new features is now much higher than the cost of *implementing* new features which is a recipe for deterioration of any early lead.

And of course everyone has their pet theory on what the "new" moat is: proprietary data, taste, distribution, etc. None of these are really new in the first place, but from my view I'm a bit torn. I am a big reader and I've always enjoyed reading about business strategy. But now that I'm at a startup, in the trenches building a product, here's what I've noticed: I don't give a shit about moats.

Warren Buffett cares about moats because he's an investor. But building products is much more an art than an engineering discipline, and when I build out features there are two things on my mind, are the details right on this feature? And does this fit with the product? [2]

Tacit knowledge is the key here and it shows up in the details, not in grand plans about moats. But details doesn't mean you can skimp on the work. People often misapply the 80/20 rule here, 80/20 isn't about leverage.

The canonical example is Apple. It's totally plausible that a number of small factors are what differentiate a MacBook pro from a – much cheaper – high end ThinkPad but these details are also clearly not sufficient. You can't get leverage in the PC market by only doing the detail work because, well, you kind of need a working computer or the whole point is moot. This is the 80%. The 20% is extracurricular. To someone who just collects features and attributes some value to them, the data will make it look like there's a hidden shortcut, but that shortcut is blocked by the fact you need to make a computer first. A lot of companies build working computers. Some even build very nice, high spec computers. But somehow most PC manufacturers manage to stay commoditized while Apple can charge significantly more for a similar computer [3].

AI makes the 80% easier than ever to copy. But the 80% maybe never mattered all that much to begin with.


## IV - The Heat Death of the Universe

No one can agree on what the new moat is. But the old moat? It's technical sophistication. You can't assume people won't come knocking your door down just because you solved a difficult technical problem. AI means your competitors will set up some [ralph loops](https://ghuntley.com/ralph/) in parallel and they will figure your product out.

And with so many incumbents hamstrung by their legal departments into using Microsoft Copilot or worse, this feels like an arbitrage opportunity.

I said I don't care about moats, but if I had to propose a new moat, it would be the things you don't do. If doing things is free, products will increasingly have their identity defined by the things they choose not to do. "No" is never free. This means AI will accelerate entropy. Which means you will need to resist more than ever before. [4]

We're living in a time when the temptation to say yes to new features has never been greater. And because of that it's never been easier to engage in conspicuous competition. The conceit of conspicuous competition is that competition keeps going. Products change but they do so in lockstep with "normal". This is why tacit knowledge matters. Any product you build with tacit knowledge can be copied once it's built. But your copier will then want a leg up. And in trying to do so, they will dilute what makes your product great.

None of this is easy though. Saying no hurts. And with AI it will hurt more than ever because you will have to say no to ideas you know are good ideas, not because they are too expensive, but because they don't fit with your product identity. But unless you say no, you stand for nothing. It will be much easier to just say yes. But that's how you lose your identity.

The other thing about saying no is that it requires risk. Saying yes feels risk free while saying no feels risky. Using no effectively requires you have some tacit knowledge, it's hard to justify a no with "our competitors are getting good metrics out of this but it doesn't *feel like us*", but I think this is what taste is. Tacit knowledge about what your product is and isn't and why that matters.

It's risky also because your intuition may be wrong and your competitors may be right. But the alternative is much worse.

You don't need to say no to features for lack of development time. Neither do your competitors. And so your competitors will be able to copy your features. Often they'll be able to do it overnight. And you, in turn, will be able to copy your competitors' features.

One sign this is a real temptation is how often I see messaging like this on social media. Want a great product idea? Clone a SaaS product that is moving slowly using AI tools. Our company is great because we can absorb features our competitors have so quickly. There are no limits, you can build anything and everything. We can all be the Jones'.

This is the heat death of the universe.

[1] - An interesting thing to note about some of these brands is how they've handled the forces of competitive entropy over time. Red Bull has introduced many more flavors, some of which I vastly prefer over the original Red Bull. But to be honest, I don't see them much different from other energy drinks. But the "energy drink" category also seems largely pioneered by them. For Ikea, the in-store experience still feels unique to me, but I can buy similar furniture at Target, Walmart, or online at Amazon or Wayfair. And there's no Ikea near me, so the primary way I'd get it is through shipping. For me, it's not very differentiated at all in this sense.

[2] - These two items probably roughly fit under the category of "taste" but I find it to be a meaninglessly vague term. And I don't think that every definition of taste makes a reasonable moat. Visual aesthetic is not that hard to copy, and I think taste is really hard at scale: There's a book called *Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments* and it's about how big tech companies A/B test features at scale. Many large tech companies are able to run hundreds of experiments a day, but what's more notable for our purposes is that these experiments appear to be very important. Most experiments are ideas someone thought was a good idea, but the success rates are abysmal. Many make the product perform worse on the metrics the experiment cares about. Many others have next to no effect. Only a minority of seemingly good ideas are actually good. I suspect the numbers are probably in part do to how well optimized a large tech company is. They found product market fit a long time ago and it's probably much harder to make improvements while avoiding damaging the core value of the product as a side-effect. This or perhaps most big tech companies have horrible taste! I'd believe this. But in any sense, this whole process seems very clearly something that AI would be good at.

[3] - I honestly know very little about Apple and could perhaps have chosen a less cliche example I knew more about. It's pretty clear Apple has more than just intangibles in their favor. Their new hardware is incredibly good and Apple M chips in particular probably do a lot of heavy lifting. That said, I think it's fairly uncontroversial to say people value Apple's design prowess.

[4] - To be absolutely clear. I am not suggesting you resist AI or moving quickly. Choosing to do this seems like its own kind of competitive suicide. Just that you are disciplined in the process.
